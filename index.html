3.1   INTRODUCTION

In unit I, we have discussed charges, whether free or bound, which are at rest. In this part, we will study charges in motion. To begin with, we will discuss some phenomena associated with charges in motion and study a few laws governing the movement of charges.

3.1.1   ELECTRIC CURRENT

The branch of physics which deals with charges at rest is called electrostatics.

Potential difference helps in flow of charges

Consider two bodies A and B having potentials of +5V and +3V respectively as shown in Fig. 5.1 (i). Clearly, body A is at higher potential than the body B.

If the two bodies are connected through a wire as shown in Fig. 5.1 (ii), then free electrons will *flow from body B to body A (conventional current is from A to B). This flow of current (i.e., charges moving in a definite direction) will continue till the two bodies attain the same potential.

Once the two bodies attain the same potential, current flow ceases. Therefore, we arrive at a very important conclusion that current will flow in a conductor or circuit if potential difference exists. If no p.d. exists between two points in a circuit, no current will flow between these points.

The branch of physics which deals with charges in motion is called current electricity.

 

3.1.2   Steady current and Varying current

The electric current through any cross section of a conductor is defined as the rate of flow of electric charge through that cross section. If the magnitude of the current does not vary with time, it is known as a steady current and if the magnitude varies with time, it is called a varying current.

3.3   ELECTRIC CURRENT

3.3.1   Conventional flow of current

The conventional direction of current is taken as the direction of flow of positive charge, or opposite to the direction of flow of negative charge. Although we always assign a direction to a current, it is a scalar quantity and not a vector quantity. Note the following points.

 1. The direction of current flow merely gives the direction of flow of positive charge.

 2. Electric current does not obey the triangle law of vector addition.

The flow of charge in a definite direction in a conductor is called electric current. The electric current is measured by the flow of charge through any cross-section  of the conductor in a unit time.
